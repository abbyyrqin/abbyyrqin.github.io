About

Hi there, I am Abby, an M.Phil. student from Hong Kong Baptist University. Born and raised in mainland China, I came to Hong Kong to pursue higher education in the summer of 2014, at the age of 18. From a skeptical onlooker in the umbrella movement to a passionate participant in the anti-extradition bill movement, I have been totally “reconfigured” by my experiences in this city.

Startled by my own transformation, I begin to wonder whether such change prevails among new mainland Chinese immigrants in Hong Kong, hence my first research on new immigrants’ information consumption and political attitudes. Within my expectation, new immigrants’ political attitudes are associated with their information practices. Yet to my surprise, after coming to Hong Kong, a city with a relatively free information environment, many mainland Chinese immigrants still opt for censored mainland Chinese information sources. This anomaly spurs me to investigate the driving forces behind their information consumption in my ongoing dissertation “The inertia of being censored”.

Despite all my commitment to the collective actions aiming at preserving the freedom of Hong Kong, my beloved city, I am aware of the limitations of our social movement. It is racist, prioritizing Cantonese-speaking Chinese, alienating mainland Chinese migrants, and distanced from the South-East Asian migrants who constitute a quite large proportion of Hong Kong’s population. Our social movement is also detached from the socio-economic realities of Hong Kong, citing the hollow terms of freedom and democracy, while keeping reticent about the city’s devastating internal wealth gap and its external exploitation of peripheral regions. Such ambivalence once kept me from protests and trapped me in endless self-reflection and self-doubt, until the National Security Law woke me up like a slap in the face. I have realized that there is no perfect social movement - the world will not be made a utopia at one go. Thus, we are only left with two choices – try our best to pursue a partial change, or indulge in designing an impossible blueprint for an impeccable social movement.

Recently, I am continuing my research about censorship and propaganda, while exploring the field of cultural studies through a project about Chinese students and workers’ challenge to DingTalk, an office tool developed by Alibaba, or an emblem of a post-socialist neoliberal surveillance state. I am a patron and zealous follower of the Revolutionary Left Radio. The books I am currently reading include The Great Transformation by Karl Polanyi and the online book On Necrocapitalism.
